SECTION 4 — TRANSCRIPT

[Formal, measured academic tone. Pace: moderate and clear.]

Lecturer: Good morning, everyone. Today's lecture is on a topic that I think you'll find directly relevant to your own lives — the psychology of decision-making, and specifically, how cognitive biases affect the choices we make every day.

Now, let's start with a simple question: how many decisions do you think you make in a day? You might guess a hundred, maybe two hundred. In fact, research suggests that the average adult makes approximately 35,000 decisions every single day. That's an enormous number. Most of these are small and automatic — what to eat for breakfast, which route to take to work — but some of them are significant and have lasting consequences.

Given the sheer volume of decisions we face, it would be impossible for us to carefully analyse every single one. Our brains simply don't have the time or the energy. So what do we do? We take shortcuts. In psychology, these mental shortcuts are called heuristics. Heuristics allow us to make rapid judgements without having to process every piece of available information. And most of the time, they work perfectly well. The main reason people use these shortcuts is to save time — our brains are essentially built for efficiency.

However — and this is the important part — sometimes these shortcuts lead us astray. When heuristics produce systematic errors in thinking, we call those errors cognitive biases. And that's what I want to focus on today. I'm going to talk about four of the most common and well-studied cognitive biases.

The first is what psychologists call the anchoring effect. This is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information you encounter when making a decision. Let me give you an example. Imagine you walk into a shop and you see a jacket with a price tag of £500. Then you see it's been reduced to £250. You might think, "What a bargain!" But is it really? The original price — the £500 — acts as an anchor. It influences your perception of the reduced price, even though £250 might still be more than the jacket is worth.

This effect has been demonstrated in many contexts. One classic study looked at behaviour in supermarkets. Researchers found that when an expensive wine was placed on the shelf next to a cheap wine — a moderately priced bottle, sorry — when an expensive wine was placed next to a moderately priced bottle, customers were more likely to buy the moderate one, perceiving it as good value. The expensive wine served as the anchor.

The second bias I want to discuss is confirmation bias. This is perhaps one of the most powerful biases of all. Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that supports your existing beliefs or hypotheses — while at the same time ignoring or downplaying evidence that contradicts them.

We see this everywhere — in politics, in the media, in everyday arguments. But here's what's particularly concerning: even professionals who are trained to be objective are affected by it. Research has shown that doctors, for example, can sometimes focus on symptoms that confirm their initial diagnosis and overlook symptoms that might point to a different condition. So this is not just something that happens to ordinary people making casual decisions — it affects experts too.

The third bias is the availability heuristic. This is the tendency to judge the probability or frequency of an event based on how easily examples come to mind. If you can think of something quickly, you assume it must be common or likely. This is why, for instance, many people are more afraid of flying than of driving, even though statistically, driving is far more dangerous. Plane crashes are dramatic and receive extensive media coverage, so examples of them are readily available in our memory. Car accidents, on the other hand, are so common that they rarely make the news, so we underestimate the risk.

Finally, let's look at loss aversion. This concept comes from the work of the psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky. Loss aversion describes the observation that people tend to feel the pain of losing something more intensely than they feel the pleasure of gaining something of equal value. In other words, losing £50 feels worse than finding £50 feels good.

This has huge implications for how we make decisions. It explains why people often stick with the status quo, even when change might benefit them — because the potential losses loom larger than the potential gains. It's also why people hold on to bad investments, hoping to avoid the pain of a definite loss.

So, what can we do about all of this? Well, the first step is simply awareness. Now that you know about these biases, you can start to recognise them in your own thinking. My suggestion for you — and this is something I encourage all my students to do — is to question your assumptions. When you're making an important decision, pause and ask yourself: Am I being influenced by an anchor? Am I only looking for evidence that supports what I already believe? Am I judging risk based on what's most memorable rather than what's most likely?

Critical thinking is a skill, and like any skill, it improves with practice. The more you question your own reasoning, the better your decisions will become.

Right, that's all for today. Next week, we'll be looking at group decision-making and the phenomenon known as "groupthink." If you have any questions, I'll be here for the next ten minutes. Thank you.

Urban farming — the practice of growing food within city boundaries — has expanded rapidly over the past two decades. What was once considered an unusual hobby for a few enthusiastic gardeners has become a serious movement with the potential to reshape how cities feed their populations. According to the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization, urban and peri-urban agriculture already supplies food to approximately 800 million people worldwide, and this number continues to grow as more cities embrace the concept.

B   The reasons behind this growth are not difficult to understand. As global populations become increasingly concentrated in urban areas — with projections suggesting that nearly 70 percent of the world's population will live in cities by 2050 — the question of how to provide fresh, affordable food to city residents has become urgent. Traditional agricultural supply chains, which transport produce over hundreds or even thousands of kilometres, are expensive, energy-intensive, and contribute significantly to greenhouse gas emissions. Urban farming offers a compelling alternative by reducing the distance between where food is grown and where it is consumed. This concept, sometimes called "food miles reduction," means that fruits and vegetables can reach consumers within hours of being harvested, rather than days or weeks.

C   Beyond its environmental advantages, urban farming delivers remarkable social benefits. In Detroit, a city that experienced severe economic decline in the early 2000s, community gardens and urban farms have transformed vacant lots into productive green spaces. Organisations such as the Michigan Urban Farming Initiative have brought neighbours together, provided job training for unemployed residents, and created a renewed sense of pride in neighbourhoods that were previously neglected. Similarly, in London, projects like Capital Growth have established more than 2,800 food-growing spaces across the city, many of which serve as gathering points for diverse communities. These gardens do far more than produce vegetables; they build social connections, improve mental health, and give residents a sense of ownership over their local environment.

D   Singapore offers perhaps the most striking example of how urban farming can be integrated into a modern city's food strategy. The small island nation imports over 90 percent of its food, making it highly vulnerable to disruptions in global supply chains. In response, the Singapore government launched its "30 by 30" initiative, which aims to produce 30 percent of the country's nutritional needs locally by the year 2030. To achieve this ambitious goal, Singapore has invested heavily in vertical farming — a method in which crops are grown in stacked layers inside climate-controlled buildings. Companies like Sky Greens operate multi-storey growing systems that use minimal water and no soil, relying instead on nutrient-rich solutions and LED lighting. These high-technology farms can produce several times more food per square metre than conventional farms, making them ideally suited to a country where land is extremely scarce.

E   Despite its clear promise, urban farming faces a number of significant challenges. The most obvious is space. In densely populated cities, land is expensive and highly sought after by property developers, businesses, and housing authorities. Convincing city planners to allocate valuable land for food production rather than commercial or residential development requires strong evidence of economic and social returns. Soil quality presents another obstacle: many urban sites are contaminated with heavy metals, industrial chemicals, or construction waste, making the ground unsuitable for growing edible crops without extensive and costly remediation. Furthermore, local regulations and zoning laws in many cities were not designed with agriculture in mind. Farmers may struggle to obtain the necessary permits to keep livestock, install irrigation systems, or sell their produce at local markets.

F   Nevertheless, the momentum behind urban farming shows no sign of slowing. Advances in technology — including hydroponics, aquaponics, and automated growing systems — are making it possible to farm in places that would have been unimaginable just a generation ago. Meanwhile, growing consumer demand for locally produced, organic food is creating new market opportunities for urban growers. Governments, too, are beginning to recognise the strategic importance of local food production, particularly in the wake of supply chain disruptions caused by events such as the COVID-19 pandemic. While urban farming is unlikely to replace conventional agriculture entirely, it is increasingly clear that it will play a vital and expanding role in feeding the cities of the future.

Sleep and Academic Performance: What the Research Shows

A   It is no secret that university students are among the most sleep-deprived groups in modern society. A combination of academic pressures, social activities, part-time employment, and the constant availability of digital entertainment means that many students regularly sleep far less than the recommended seven to nine hours per night. A large-scale survey conducted by the American College Health Association in 2019 found that over 60 percent of university students described their sleep quality as "poor" or "fairly poor," and more than a quarter reported that sleep difficulties had negatively affected their academic performance within the previous twelve months. These figures are not unique to the United States; similar patterns have been observed in studies from the United Kingdom, Australia, South Korea, and numerous other countries.

B   The relationship between sleep and cognitive function has been extensively studied, and the findings are remarkably consistent. Research published in the journal Sleep by Dr. Matthew Walker and his colleagues at the University of California, Berkeley, demonstrated that students who slept fewer than six hours per night in the week before an examination scored, on average, 10 percent lower than those who maintained a regular sleep schedule of seven to eight hours. Walker's research highlighted the critical role of sleep in memory consolidation — the process by which the brain transfers information from short-term to long-term memory. During deep sleep, the hippocampus replays the day's learning experiences, strengthening neural connections and making information easier to retrieve later. When sleep is curtailed, this process is interrupted, and students may find that material they studied thoroughly the night before feels unfamiliar during the exam.

C   Interestingly, not all sleep is equal when it comes to learning. Research has shown that different stages of sleep serve different cognitive functions. Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep, which occurs primarily in the later hours of a full night's sleep, is particularly important for creative problem-solving and the integration of complex ideas. Slow-wave sleep, which dominates the first half of the night, is more closely associated with the retention of factual information and vocabulary. This has practical implications for students: those who go to bed very late and wake up early may get adequate slow-wave sleep but miss much of their REM sleep, potentially impairing their ability to think critically and make connections between different concepts. Dr. Sara Mednick, a sleep researcher at the University of California, Irvine, has noted that understanding these sleep stages could help students make more informed decisions about their study schedules.

D   The topic of napping has generated considerable interest among researchers in recent years. A study conducted by Mednick and her team found that a 60- to 90-minute afternoon nap containing both slow-wave and REM sleep could improve performance on learning tasks by as much as 20 percent. Shorter naps of 15 to 20 minutes, while less restorative, were also found to boost alertness and reduce errors on attention-based tasks. However, Mednick cautioned that napping is not a substitute for a full night's sleep. Students who rely on naps to compensate for chronic sleep deprivation still show lower overall cognitive performance compared to those who sleep adequately at night. Furthermore, napping too late in the day — after 3:00 p.m. — can interfere with nighttime sleep, creating a cycle of poor sleep habits that is difficult to break.

E   One factor that makes sleep management particularly challenging for students is the natural shift in circadian rhythms that occurs during adolescence and young adulthood. Research by Dr. Mary Carskadon at Brown University has demonstrated that during this period, the body's internal clock shifts later, meaning that young people naturally feel sleepy later at night and find it difficult to wake up early in the morning. This biological tendency directly conflicts with traditional university timetables, which often schedule lectures and examinations in the early morning. Carskadon has argued that universities should consider offering more flexible scheduling options to accommodate students' natural sleep patterns, a recommendation that has been adopted by a small but growing number of institutions worldwide.

F   Given the strength of the evidence, sleep scientists have put forward several practical recommendations for students seeking to improve both their sleep and their academic results. First, they advise maintaining a consistent sleep schedule — going to bed and waking up at roughly the same time each day, including weekends. Second, they recommend limiting exposure to screens and blue light in the hour before bedtime, as research has shown that the light emitted by phones and laptops suppresses the production of melatonin, a hormone that signals to the body that it is time to sleep. Third, they suggest that students avoid caffeine after mid-afternoon, as its stimulating effects can last for six hours or more. Finally, they encourage students to view sleep not as a luxury that can be sacrificed during busy periods, but as a fundamental component of academic success — one that is just as important as attending lectures or completing assignments.

The Economics of Happiness: Can Money Buy Well-Being?

A   Few questions have occupied economists, philosophers, and psychologists as persistently as the relationship between wealth and happiness. Common sense suggests a straightforward connection: more money means greater comfort, security, and opportunity, all of which should logically lead to higher levels of personal satisfaction. Yet the empirical evidence tells a far more complicated story — one that challenges deeply held assumptions about the purpose of economic growth and the nature of human contentment.

B   The modern academic debate on this subject was largely ignited by the economist Richard Easterlin, who in 1974 published a landmark paper examining the relationship between national income and reported happiness across countries and over time. Easterlin observed a puzzling pattern: while wealthier individuals within a given country tended to report higher life satisfaction than poorer individuals, increases in a country's average income over several decades did not produce corresponding increases in average happiness. This finding, which became known as the Easterlin Paradox, suggested that beyond a certain threshold of income — enough to meet basic needs such as food, shelter, healthcare, and education — additional wealth contributed surprisingly little to overall well-being. Easterlin proposed that this paradox could be explained by the fact that people evaluate their economic situation not in absolute terms, but relative to the people around them.

C   The concept of relative wealth has been explored extensively by subsequent researchers. Professor Andrew Clark of the Paris School of Economics conducted a series of studies demonstrating that an individual's satisfaction with their income depends heavily on how that income compares to the earnings of their peers, colleagues, and neighbours. In one well-known experiment, participants were asked to choose between two scenarios: earning $50,000 per year while their neighbours earned $25,000, or earning $100,000 per year while their neighbours earned $250,000. A significant majority chose the first option, despite it representing a substantially lower absolute income. Clark argued that this preference reveals a fundamental feature of human psychology: our sense of financial well-being is shaped more by social comparison than by the actual amount of money we possess. This finding has been replicated across cultures, though its strength varies — some researchers, including Dr. Shigehiro Oishi of the University of Virginia, have found that the effect of social comparison on happiness is somewhat weaker in collectivist societies than in individualist ones.

D   Another key concept in understanding the relationship between money and happiness is hedonic adaptation — the well-documented tendency for people to return to a baseline level of happiness after experiencing significant positive or negative life changes. Psychologist Dr. Daniel Kahneman, a Nobel laureate in Economics, studied this phenomenon extensively and found that events people expect will dramatically improve their happiness — such as receiving a large salary increase or purchasing a new home — typically produce only a temporary boost in mood. Within months, people adjust to their new circumstances and their satisfaction returns to approximately where it was before. Kahneman described this process as a "hedonic treadmill," suggesting that people are continually striving for more without experiencing lasting gains in happiness. His research, conducted with Dr. Angus Deaton of Princeton University, also identified a specific income threshold in the United States: emotional well-being — the day-to-day experience of positive and negative feelings — stopped increasing beyond an annual household income of approximately $75,000 (in 2010 dollars), even though people's broader evaluation of their lives continued to rise with income beyond that point.

E   Not all researchers agree with the conclusions drawn by Easterlin and Kahneman. In 2013, economists Betsey Stevenson and Justin Wolfers of the University of Michigan published an influential analysis using data from 155 countries, arguing that the Easterlin Paradox does not hold when more recent and comprehensive data is considered. Stevenson and Wolfers found a clear, positive, and roughly logarithmic relationship between income and life satisfaction at every level of income, both within and across countries. They contended that while the marginal gain in happiness decreases as income rises — meaning that going from $10,000 to $20,000 brings more happiness than going from $100,000 to $110,000 — there is no clear point at which more money ceases to matter entirely. Their work was supported by a 2021 study by Dr. Matthew Killingsworth of the University of Pennsylvania, who used a smartphone-based experience sampling method to collect over one million real-time reports of well-being and found that happiness continued to rise with income well beyond the $75,000 threshold identified by Kahneman and Deaton.

F   These academic debates have significant implications for public policy. If happiness does not increase indefinitely with national income, as Easterlin originally suggested, then governments might be better served by focusing on reducing inequality, strengthening social services, and investing in areas such as mental health, community development, and work-life balance rather than pursuing economic growth as an end in itself. The small Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan famously adopted this philosophy when it introduced the concept of Gross National Happiness (GNH) as an alternative to Gross Domestic Product (GDP), measuring national progress through indicators such as psychological well-being, health, education, cultural resilience, and environmental sustainability. Several other nations, including New Zealand and Scotland, have since adopted well-being frameworks that similarly aim to look beyond purely economic measures of success.

G   On the other hand, if Stevenson and Wolfers are correct that income continues to improve life satisfaction at all levels, then economic growth remains a legitimate and important policy objective — though it should ideally be pursued alongside measures that promote equitable distribution. What both sides of the debate tend to agree on, however, is that the relationship between money and happiness is far from simple. Factors such as personal relationships, meaningful work, physical health, a sense of autonomy, and connection to community consistently emerge as powerful predictors of well-being, often rivalling or surpassing the influence of income. The challenge for policymakers and individuals alike is to recognise that while financial security is undoubtedly important, it represents only one piece of a much larger and more complex puzzle.

Examiner Notes — All Four Skills

Listening: Key Traps and Marking Notes

Spelling Requirements:

  • Spelling must be correct for full marks. Both British and American spellings are accepted (centre/center, colour/color).
  • Proper nouns must be capitalised, though markers generally accept lowercase.
  • Numbers can be written as digits or words (e.g., 2 or two), but digits are strongly recommended to avoid spelling errors.

Key Traps in This Exam:

  • Section 1, Q2: The agent mentions multiple prices for different properties. Students must associate the correct price with the correct property — the first property discussed is £475.
  • Section 2, Q18: Date comprehension — eighteen eighty-five vs. eighteen fifty-five. Students must distinguish clearly between similar-sounding decades.
  • Section 3, Q26–30: Matching requires tracking three speakers. Students who lose track of who is speaking will assign statements to the wrong person.
  • Section 4, Q31–35: Academic vocabulary in the summary completion. Students need to recognise technical terms (loss aversion, anchoring effect) and write them accurately.

Band 5 vs. Band 7 Listening Differences:

  • Band 5 (16–19 correct): Student can follow straightforward conversations (Sections 1–2) but struggles with faster speech and academic content (Sections 3–4). Typically gets 7–8 correct in Sections 1–2 but only 4–5 in Sections 3–4.
  • Band 7 (30–32 correct): Student handles all sections competently. May lose marks on tricky distractors or detailed academic vocabulary, but demonstrates consistent comprehension across all difficulty levels.

Reading: Strategies and Marking Notes

True/False/Not Given vs. Yes/No/Not Given:

  • T/F/NG tests whether factual information in the passage matches the statement.
  • Y/N/NG tests whether the statement matches the author's views or claims.
  • Students frequently confuse these. In this exam, Passage 1 uses T/F/NG (facts) and Passage 2 uses Y/N/NG (opinions).

Strategies per Passage:

  • Passage 1 (Urban Farming): The easiest passage. Spend no more than 15 minutes. T/F/NG questions follow passage order. Read the statements first, then scan for keywords.
  • Passage 2 (Sleep): Medium difficulty. Allow 20 minutes. The matching information questions (Q14–18) do NOT follow passage order — read all five statements first, then work through paragraphs.
  • Passage 3 (Economics of Happiness): The hardest passage with the most abstract content. Allow 25 minutes. The sentence completions require precise vocabulary from the passage. The matching features require remembering which researcher said what — annotate names as you read.

Time Management:

  • Total: 60 minutes for 40 questions across 3 passages.
  • Recommended split: 15 + 20 + 25 minutes.
  • Never spend more than 2 minutes on a single question. Mark it and return if time allows.
  • Transfer answers as you go (unlike Listening, there is no extra transfer time).

Common General Errors:

  • Writing more words than the word limit allows (answers are marked wrong regardless of accuracy).
  • For summary completion from a word list, using a word not in the list.
  • Choosing NOT GIVEN as a "safe" answer when unsure — students overuse this option.

Speaking: What Examiners Look For

Part 1 (Introduction & Interview, 4–5 minutes):

  • Familiar topics: home, studies, hobbies, daily routine.
  • Examiners assess fluency and willingness to extend answers beyond one-word responses.
  • Band 5: Gives short, simple answers. Frequent pauses to search for words. Limited range of topics discussed comfortably.
  • Band 7: Speaks at length without noticeable effort. Uses a range of vocabulary naturally. Answers are well-developed with relevant detail.

Part 2 (Individual Long Turn, 3–4 minutes):

  • 1-minute preparation + 1–2 minute talk on a cue card topic.
  • Examiners assess ability to organise and sustain a monologue.
  • Band 5: May only speak for 30–60 seconds. Loses track of the topic. Relies on simple, repetitive structures.
  • Band 7: Speaks for the full 2 minutes fluently. Covers all bullet points on the cue card. Uses descriptive language and varied structures.

Part 3 (Two-Way Discussion, 4–5 minutes):

  • Abstract questions related to the Part 2 topic.
  • Examiners assess ability to discuss abstract ideas, speculate, and justify opinions.
  • Band 5: Struggles with abstract topics. Answers are brief and lack depth. Cannot explain or justify views clearly.
  • Band 7: Engages with abstract ideas confidently. Provides extended answers with examples. Can speculate about causes, consequences, and solutions. Uses a range of discourse markers.

Marking Criteria (each scored 0–9): 1. Fluency and Coherence: Speaks smoothly with logical development. Self-correction is natural, not disruptive. 2. Lexical Resource: Range and precision of vocabulary. Ability to paraphrase when needed. 3. Grammatical Range and Accuracy: Uses a variety of structures correctly. Errors do not impede communication. 4. Pronunciation: Clear, intelligible speech. Uses features of connected speech, stress, and intonation naturally.

WARNING: Memorised answers are penalised. Examiners are trained to detect rehearsed responses and will redirect the conversation. Naturalness is valued over perfection.

Improvement Notes by Band Level

Band 5.0–5.5: Building Foundations

Current Profile: You understand the gist of most texts and conversations but miss details. Your writing contains frequent errors that sometimes obscure meaning. Speaking is hesitant with limited vocabulary.

What to Work On:

1. Vocabulary Expansion (Priority):

  • Learn 10 new academic words per day using spaced repetition (Anki, Quizlet).
  • Focus on the Academic Word List (AWL) — 570 word families that appear across all IELTS topics.
  • Don't just learn definitions — learn collocations (make a decision not do a decision, conduct research not make research).

2. Grammar Essentials:

  • Master the present simple, past simple, and present perfect — these are the most common tenses in IELTS and the most error-prone at this level.
  • Practice complex sentences with because, although, while, if.
  • Aim for 60% accuracy in complex sentences rather than 100% simple sentences — the examiner rewards attempt at range.

3. Listening Skills:

  • Listen to English for at least 30 minutes daily (podcasts, BBC Learning English, TED Talks).
  • Practice prediction: before listening, look at the questions and predict what type of answer is needed (name, number, place).
  • Focus on Sections 1 and 2 first — these are the most accessible and build confidence.

4. Reading Skills:

  • Start with Passage 1 in every practice test — aim for 10/13 correct.
  • Practice skimming (reading for gist) and scanning (searching for specific information).
  • Don't read every word. Learn to identify topic sentences (usually the first sentence of each paragraph).

5. Writing:

  • Write one Task 2 essay per week. Focus on structure: introduction → body paragraph 1 → body paragraph 2 → conclusion.
  • Memorise 5 essay structures (opinion, discussion, advantage/disadvantage, problem/solution, two-part question) and practise each.
  • Always count your words. Being under 250 words in Task 2 guarantees a penalty.

6. Speaking:

  • Record yourself speaking for 2 minutes on random topics daily.
  • Listen back and note pauses, repetitions, and simple vocabulary.
  • Learn 3–5 "filler phrases" to buy thinking time naturally: That's an interesting question..., I'd say that..., From my perspective...

Practice Activities:

  • Complete one full practice test every two weeks under timed conditions.
  • Keep an error log: write down every mistake and review weekly.
  • Find a study partner for speaking practice at least twice a week.

Realistic Timeline to Band 6.0: 2–4 months of consistent daily study (1.5–2 hours per day).

---

Band 6.0–6.5: Bridging to Competent User

Current Profile: You handle most situations adequately but lack precision and sophistication. Your writing is organised but may feel formulaic. You can discuss familiar topics fluently but struggle with abstract or complex ideas.

What to Work On:

1. Vocabulary Sophistication:

  • Move beyond the AWL to topic-specific vocabulary (environment, technology, education, health, globalisation).
  • Learn synonyms and paraphrasing skills — the ability to express the same idea in different words is essential for Band 7.
  • Study word formation: economy → economic → economically → economist → economise.

2. Grammar Refinement:

  • Introduce advanced structures: conditionals (2nd and 3rd), passive voice, relative clauses, participial phrases.
  • Focus on accuracy with articles (a/an/the) — article errors are extremely common at this level and easy to reduce.
  • Practice mixing sentence lengths: short for impact, long for explanation.

3. Listening Improvement:

  • Focus on Sections 3 and 4 — these are where Band 6 students lose the most marks.
  • Practice note-taking while listening (don't try to write full sentences).
  • Listen to academic lectures (university podcasts, Coursera previews) to build tolerance for academic speech patterns.

4. Reading Speed and Accuracy:

  • You should be finishing all 40 questions with 5 minutes to spare. If not, you're reading too slowly.
  • For T/F/NG: learn to identify the difference between "the passage says the opposite" (FALSE) and "the passage doesn't discuss this" (NOT GIVEN). This distinction is the single biggest source of errors at Band 6.
  • For Passage 3: read the questions before the passage to know what to look for.

5. Writing:

  • Your biggest gap is likely in Task Achievement/Response — you answer the question but don't develop your ideas enough.
  • For every main idea, provide: explanation + example + result/consequence.
  • Vary your linking words: replace Firstly... Secondly... Finally with To begin with... Another key consideration... Perhaps most significantly...
  • Task 1: Always include an overview paragraph as your second paragraph — this is essential for Band 7.

6. Speaking:

  • Extend answers with reasons and examples without being prompted.
  • In Part 3, practice speculating: It might be because..., One possible explanation is..., This could lead to...
  • Work on pronunciation features: word stress (phoPHOgraphy not phoTOGraphy), sentence stress, and connected speech.

Practice Activities:

  • Complete one full practice test every week under strict exam conditions.
  • After each test, spend equal time analysing your errors as you spent taking the test.
  • Read one article from The Guardian, The Economist, or New Scientist daily — highlight useful vocabulary and structures.
  • Write a timed essay (40 minutes) twice per week and have it evaluated (teacher, study partner, or online marking service).

Realistic Timeline to Band 7.0: 3–6 months of focused daily study (2 hours per day). The jump from 6.5 to 7.0 is widely considered the hardest half-band jump in IELTS.

Band 7.0+: Maintenance and Advanced Strategies

Current Profile: You are a competent user of English with good command of the language. You handle complex and detailed argumentation well, though you may make occasional errors. Your goal is to maintain consistency and push toward 7.5 or 8.0.

Maintenance Strategies:

1. Consistency Is Everything:

  • At Band 7+, the difference between a good day and a bad day can be a full band. The goal is not to learn new skills but to perform your existing skills reliably under pressure.
  • Take a full practice test once every 2 weeks to maintain test stamina.
  • Focus on your weakest skill — your overall band is dragged down by your lowest score.

2. Advanced Reading:

  • Passage 3 should be your training ground. Read academic journals and practise identifying argumentation structure: thesis → evidence → counter-argument → rebuttal.
  • For matching features and matching headings, train yourself to distinguish between main ideas and supporting details.
  • Target: 35+ correct consistently.

3. Advanced Listening:

  • Listen to content at 1.25× speed during practice. When you return to normal speed, it will feel slower and you'll catch more detail.
  • Focus on signpost language in Section 4: However, On the other hand, What's particularly interesting is... — these phrases signal key information.
  • Target: 33+ correct consistently.

4. Advanced Writing:

  • For Band 8 in Task 2: Your essay should read like a well-constructed argument, not a formulaic template. Examiners at this level can spot memorised phrases instantly.
  • Develop a personal writing style. Use rhetorical questions, concessions (While it is true that..., nevertheless...), and nuanced positions (rather than simply agreeing or disagreeing, explore the complexity).
  • For Task 1: master all chart types. The most commonly underprepared are maps (describing changes over time) and process diagrams (using sequential passive structures).
  • Use less common vocabulary naturally: instead of important, try pivotal, instrumental, paramount. But only if you're confident in the usage — misusing sophisticated vocabulary is worse than using simple vocabulary correctly.

5. Advanced Speaking:

  • Work on discourse management in Part 3: structuring long answers with clear topic sentences and smooth transitions.
  • Develop the ability to self-correct naturally without disrupting fluency.
  • Expand your idiomatic range: a double-edged sword, the tip of the iceberg, a level playing field — but use sparingly and accurately.
  • For pronunciation at Band 8: focus on intonation patterns (rising for lists, falling for statements) and weak forms (to → /tə/, and → /ən/).

6. Test-Day Strategies:

  • Sleep well the night before. Cognitive performance drops significantly with even mild sleep deprivation (as Passage 2 explains!).
  • In Listening, use the 30-second check time between sections to preview the next section's questions — don't review what you've already heard.
  • In Reading, if you finish early, prioritise checking your T/F/NG and Y/N/NG answers — these have the highest error rate even for strong candidates.
  • In Writing, spend 5 minutes planning each task before writing. A clear plan produces a better-organised essay than a longer essay without structure.
  • In Speaking, treat it as a conversation, not an interrogation. Smile, make eye contact, and engage naturally.

Target Scores for Band 7.5–8.0:

  • Listening: 35–38 raw score
  • Reading: 35–38 raw score
  • Writing: Consistent 7.0+ across all four criteria in both tasks
  • Speaking: Natural fluency with sophisticated vocabulary and near-error-free grammar
